The adoption is complete. You have a carrier, a litter box, and a cat who is currently hiding under your bed refusing to come out. Congratulations — this is normal.

Rescue cats from Cyprus have usually experienced some combination of street life, shelter overcrowding, medical treatment, and transport stress. The first few weeks in a new home are a transition period that requires patience, structure, and a small amount of preparation. Most new adopters get the emotional part right and underestimate the logistical part.

Here is what the first 30 days actually look like, and what you need to have in place.

## Days 1 to 3: The Hiding Phase

Your cat will hide. This is not rejection. It is a survival instinct. A rescue cat in a new environment is processing unfamiliar smells, sounds, and spatial layout all at once. Hiding is how they regulate that sensory overload.

Set up a single room as the cat's base — ideally a quiet room with a door that closes. Place the litter box, food, water, and a hiding spot (a cardboard box with a hole cut in it works perfectly) in this room. Leave the cat alone. Visit briefly to refresh food and water, speak quietly, and leave. Do not force interaction.

Most cats begin to emerge within two to five days. Some take longer. A cat that hides for a full week is not unusual for a former stray.

## Days 3 to 14: Exploration and Routine

Once the cat begins voluntarily leaving its hiding spot, allow it to explore at its own pace. Leave the door to the base room open and let the cat choose when to investigate the rest of the home. Do not carry the cat around or place it in rooms it has not chosen to enter.

Establish a feeding routine. Cats thrive on predictability. Feed at the same times each day. Use the same food the shelter or rescue was providing for the first two weeks, then transition gradually if you want to change brands.

If the cat is not eating after 48 hours, contact your vet. Cats can develop hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease) after as few as three to four days without food, and this is a medical emergency.

## The Vet Visit

Schedule a veterinary check-up within the first week, even if the rescue organization has provided vaccination and sterilization records. Your vet should confirm vaccination status, check for parasites (common in former strays), assess dental health, and discuss any ongoing medication.

If you do not yet have a vet in Cyprus, the rescue organization that facilitated your adoption can usually recommend one. See our [guide to choosing a vet in Cyprus](/blog/choosing-a-vet-in-cyprus-what-to-look-for) for what to evaluate.

Make sure the vet updates the microchip registration to your name and contact information. This is easy to forget and critically important if the cat escapes.

## The Supply List

You need less than pet stores want you to buy.

A litter box (one per cat, plus one extra — so two for a single cat). Unscented clumping litter. Food and water bowls, ideally ceramic or stainless steel rather than plastic. A scratching post or pad. A cardboard box for hiding. A carrier for vet visits. Wet and dry food appropriate to the cat's age. A brush for grooming.

You do not need an expensive cat tree, automated feeders, or specialty toys in the first month. Keep it simple. The cat needs security, not stimulation, during the adjustment period.

## When You Need to Travel

At some point, you will need to leave your cat. This is the moment where new rescue cat owners often panic, because the cat took three weeks to stop hiding and now they are supposed to leave it with a stranger.

The best option is an in-home sitter who visits your home once or twice daily while you are away. The cat stays in its territory, maintains its routine, and does not experience the stress of a new environment on top of an already recent environmental change.

On [Tinies](/services/search), you can search for cat sitters in your area who have experience with rescue animals. The [meet-and-greet](/how-it-works) is important — have the sitter visit your home before the trip, meet the cat (even if the cat hides the entire time), and go over the routine in detail.

For trips of more than a few days, overnight in-home sitting is strongly recommended for rescue cats in their first year. Being left completely alone overnight is more stressful for a recently adopted cat than for a cat who has lived in the same home for years.

## Common Surprises

Rescue cats from Cyprus frequently carry FIV (feline immunodeficiency virus). This is not a death sentence. FIV-positive cats can live full, healthy lives indoors. They should not be housed with FIV-negative cats, and they may need more frequent veterinary monitoring. If your rescue is FIV-positive, your vet will advise on management.

Some rescue cats from street backgrounds will resource guard — hissing over food, claiming specific sleeping spots aggressively, or reacting to other animals. This is a trauma response, not a personality defect. It typically diminishes over time with consistent routine and patient handling.

Litter box avoidance in the first two weeks is common and almost always related to stress. Do not punish the cat. Ensure the box is clean, accessible, and in a quiet location. If avoidance persists beyond two weeks, consult your vet to rule out a urinary tract infection.

## The Long View

Most rescue cats settle fully into their new home within one to three months. By month three, the cat that hid under your bed for a week will probably be sleeping on your pillow. The adjustment is not always linear — there will be good days and setbacks — but the trajectory is overwhelmingly positive.

You took an animal off the street, out of a shelter, or out of a sanctuary where it was one of ninety-two cats competing for care and space. That matters. The first month is work. The years after that are the reward.
