Cyprus is one of the most popular relocation destinations for British, Northern European, and Middle Eastern expats. The climate, the cost of living relative to Western Europe, the English-speaking population, and the EU membership make it attractive for individuals and families. Many of those families include pets.

Moving a dog or cat to Cyprus is straightforward if you plan ahead. It becomes expensive and stressful if you do not. Here is the complete process, the common mistakes, and what you need to set up once you arrive.

## Before You Move: The Requirements

Every dog or cat entering Cyprus must meet the following requirements. There are no exceptions and no shortcuts.

**Microchip.** Your pet must be identified with an [ISO 11784/11785 compliant microchip](https://www.pettravel.com/information/pet-passports/cyprus-pet-import-requirements/) before any vaccinations are administered. If the microchip was implanted after the rabies vaccination, the vaccination is considered invalid and must be repeated. This catches people every year.

**Rabies vaccination.** Required for all dogs and cats entering Cyprus. The vaccine must be administered after microchipping, when the pet is at least 12 weeks old. The vaccine is valid from 21 days after administration until the expiry date indicated by the manufacturer.

**Rabies antibody titre test.** Required if you are traveling from a country not listed as an [EU-approved third country](https://www.pettravel.com/information/pet-passports/cyprus-pet-import-requirements/). This includes most countries outside the EU, UK, US, Canada, and Australia. The blood sample must be taken at least 30 days after vaccination and at least three months before travel. The test must show a neutralizing antibody titre of at least 0.5 IU/ml, performed at an EU-approved laboratory.

If you are traveling from the UK, you need an Animal Health Certificate (AHC) rather than an EU Pet Passport. UK Pet Passports are no longer accepted since Brexit.

If you are traveling from an EU country, an EU Pet Passport issued by a licensed vet in your country of residence is sufficient.

**Health certificate.** An official veterinary health certificate must be issued within 10 days of travel. This is signed by a government-authorized vet in your country of origin (APHIS-endorsed in the US, DEFRA in the UK).

**The EU 5-day rule.** For non-commercial transport, your pet must [travel with you or within five days of your arrival](https://www.petrelocation.com/country/cyprus). If your pet arrives more than five days after you, the import is reclassified as commercial, which involves stricter requirements and higher costs.

**Banned breeds.** Cyprus prohibits the import of Pit Bull Terriers, Japanese Tosas, Dogo Argentinos, and Fila Brasileiros, [including crosses of these breeds](https://www.bringfido.com/travel/international/cyprus/).

## The Timeline

Start the process at least four months before your move if you need a titre test. At least 30 days before if you are coming from an EU country or a listed third country (UK, US, Canada, Australia).

A realistic timeline from the UK looks like this: microchip (if not already done), rabies vaccination (21-day wait), AHC from a vet (within 10 days of travel), notification to Cyprus Veterinary Services at least 48 hours before arrival, and travel.

From the US, add the USDA/APHIS endorsement step, which can take additional processing time. [APHIS provides specific guidance for Cyprus](https://www.aphis.usda.gov/pet-travel/us-to-another-country-export/pet-travel-us-cyprus).

## The Cost

The total cost of moving a pet to Cyprus depends on your origin country and transport method.

From the UK, expect to pay approximately €300 to €800 per pet. This includes the AHC (around £100 to £200), microchip and vaccination if not already done (£50 to £100), airline pet transport fee (£100 to £400 depending on cabin vs cargo and airline), and miscellaneous documentation.

From the US, costs are higher due to the APHIS endorsement and longer flight. Budget €500 to €1,500 per pet, with the airline transport fee being the largest variable.

Professional pet relocation services — companies that handle the entire process for you — charge €1,000 to €3,000 or more, depending on origin and number of pets. [Pets2Fly](https://www.pets2fly.com/en/location/bringing-pets-to-cyprus/) and [PetRelocation](https://www.petrelocation.com/country/cyprus) are two providers with Cyprus-specific experience.

## Points of Entry

Pets can only enter the Republic of Cyprus through [Larnaca (LCA) and Paphos (PFO) international airports](https://www.cyexpats.com/guides/Bringing_Pets_to_Cyprus.html), or the ports of Limassol, Larnaca, and Paphos. Upon arrival, a Veterinary Officer inspects the pet and its documentation. If everything is in order, there is no quarantine.

Important: pets cannot cross the Green Line between the Republic of Cyprus and Northern Cyprus. If you are moving to North Cyprus, the entry requirements and process are [different and separate](https://www.pettravel.com/information/pet-passports/northern-cyprus-pet-import-requirements/).

## After You Arrive: Setting Up

Once you are in Cyprus with your pet, there are several things to arrange in the first few weeks.

**Find a vet.** Register with a local veterinary clinic near your home. Your pet will need a local vet for ongoing vaccinations, parasite treatment (tick and flea prevention is essential in Cyprus's warm climate), and any medical issues. See our [guide to choosing a vet in Cyprus](/blog/choosing-a-vet-in-cyprus-what-to-look-for).

**Update the microchip registration.** Ensure the microchip database record reflects your new Cyprus address and phone number. If you used a UK microchip registry, register with a local Cyprus database as well.

**Find pet care for when you travel.** As an expat, you will likely travel back home or elsewhere during holidays. Establishing a relationship with a trusted pet sitter before you need one urgently saves the panic of a last-minute search. Browse [pet sitters on Tinies](/services/search) and book a trial walk or short sitting session to test the relationship.

**Understand the heat.** If you have moved from a cooler climate, your pet needs time to acclimatize. Avoid midday walks in summer. Ensure constant access to water and shade. Be aware of hot pavement — if it is too hot for your bare foot, it is too hot for your dog's paws.

**Be aware of the stray population.** Cyprus has an estimated one million stray cats and a significant stray dog population. Your dog will encounter strays on walks. Keep your dog leashed in areas with stray populations, and ensure vaccinations are current. If you want to understand the broader context, read [Why Cyprus Has 1.5 Million Stray Cats](/blog/why-cyprus-has-15-million-stray-cats-and-what-you-can-do).

## The Expat Pet Community

One of the unexpected benefits of having a pet in Cyprus is the community it opens up. Dog walkers meet other dog walkers. Cat owners connect through local rescue organizations. Expat pet groups on Facebook are among the most active and helpful communities on the island.
