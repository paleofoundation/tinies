Rescue animals often arrive with unknown histories. Your job in week one is not to show them every room at once — it is to prove that this home is predictable, safe, and boring in the best way.

## The decompression zone

Choose one small area: a spare bedroom, bathroom, or cordoned corner of the living room. Add a bed or crate with the door open, water, food at scheduled times, and a litter tray for cats placed away from food. Block hiding hazards (loose wires, gaps behind furniture). Keep visitors minimal; excited children should sit quietly and let the animal approach.

## Shopping list

- Species-appropriate food (ask the rescue what they currently eat).
- Bowls, litter and tray or outdoor potty plan, biodegradable bags.
- ID tag with your phone number even before the animal explores outside.
- Cat: vertical space or scratching post; dog: harness and fixed-length lead for walks.
- First-aid basics and the number of a 24-hour vet.

## Routines beat random affection

Feed at the same times. Walk the same short route for dogs at first. For cats, sit in the room reading aloud — your calm voice matters more than forced cuddles. Avoid baths and nail trims until trust is obvious.

## Introducing resident pets

Follow rescue guidance. Parallel walks on neutral ground work for many dogs. For cats, scent swapping (towel rubs) before visual contact reduces fights. Never leave new and resident animals unsupervised until you have seen relaxed body language repeatedly.

## When to call the vet

Not eating for more than 24 hours (12 for young kittens), vomiting with lethargy, or difficulty breathing are urgent. Stress diarrhoea can be common; hydration matters.

You are not failing if progress is slow. You are succeeding if the animal eats, sleeps, and eventually chooses to sit nearer to you.
